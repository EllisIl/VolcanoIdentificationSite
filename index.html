VOLCANO IDENTIFICATION GUIDE

1. Identifying Volcano Shapes

Shield Volcano

Broad, dome-shaped volcano with long, sloping sides created by highly fluid lava eruptions.

Wide base and gently sloping sides are characteristic of shield volcanoes.

Stratovolcano (or Composite Volcano)

Steep-sided, symmetrical cone built by alternating layers of solidified lava flows, volcanic ash, and other volcanic debris.

Notice the steep slopes and layered structure.

Caldera Volcano

A massive, bowl-shaped depression formed from the collapse of a volcano after an eruption.

The hollowed-out bowl shape is the signature of a caldera.

2. Identifying Volcano Rocks

Texture: Intrusive vs Extrusive

Textures in volcanic rocks can be mainly classified into intrusive and extrusive. Intrusive rocks are formed when magma cools slowly beneath the Earth's surface, resulting in large, visible crystals. Extrusive rocks, on the other hand, form when lava cools quickly on the Earth's surface, often resulting in smaller crystals or a glassy texture.

Intrusive texture showing large, visible crystals. Extrusive texture with smaller crystals or a glassy appearance.

Composition: High-Iron vs High-Silica vs Intermediate

Volcanic rocks can vary based on their chemical compositions. Those with a higher iron content tend to be darker, while high-silica rocks are lighter. Intermediate rocks fall in between these two extremes.

High-Iron volcanic rock with a darker appearance. High-Silica volcanic rock with a lighter color. Intermediate volcanic rock with a color between dark and light.

Identifying Rocks Using Texture & Composition

By studying both the texture and composition of a volcanic rock, one can determine its type. For instance, a rock with an intrusive texture and high-silica composition is likely granite, while one with an extrusive texture and high-iron composition might be basalt.

3. Identifying Volcano Hazards

Lava Flows

Lava flows are streams of molten rock that pour or ooze from an erupting vent. While generally not fast-moving, they can consume everything in their path.

Common in: Shield Volcanoes

Pyroclastic Flows

A mixture of hot gas, ash, and volcanic rocks that travel rapidly due to gravity. These are extremely lethal and can destroy everything in their path.

Common in: Stratovolcanoes

Lahars

Lahars are volcanic mudflows or debris flows. They are powerful mixtures of volcanic debris and water which can move at high speeds and have immense destructive capability.

Common in: Stratovolcanoes and Shield Volcanoes when they have glaciers or significant snow.

Ash Clouds

Explosive eruptions can release ash clouds into the atmosphere. This ash can reduce visibility, disrupt aviation, and pose health risks to humans and animals.

Common in: Stratovolcanoes

Volcanic Gasses

Volcanoes can release various gases, including water vapor, CO2, and sulfur dioxide. These can affect air quality, acidify rain, and have various health implications.

Common in: All types of volcanoes

4. What to do When Visiting a Volcano

Before Visiting:

  • Research the volcano's activity.
  • Check weather conditions and potential hazards.
  • Equip yourself with protective gear like masks, goggles, and sturdy shoes.

While Visiting:

  • Stay on marked trails and respect restricted areas.
  • Avoid river valleys and low-lying areas due to the risk of lahars.
  • Pay attention to warning signs and updates from local authorities.
  • Have an emergency plan and know the evacuation routes.
